Project: Goodbye Gondola

Marni Epstein - Michael Eyer - Audrey Miller

Project Background:

Little Cottonwood Canyon (LCC) is home to world class rock climbing, skiing, and hiking. Located only fifteen minutes from Salt Lake City, UT, locals enjoy year-round access to rock climbing, and more specifically, bouldering. LCC is also a popular destination for climbers from all around the world due to its unique granite climbing. Presently, the Utah Department of Transportation is debating a final transportation recommendation to address traffic in Little Cottonwood Canyon. In the summer of 2022, they identified a gondola from the base of the canyon to Snowbird and Alta, two private ski resorts, as the preferred alternative. This gondola would have extreme and irrevocable impacts on Little Cottonwood Canyon, destroying boulders that climbers have cherished for years. Goodbye Gondola is a visualization displaying the potential boulders that will be affected by the gondola construction, providing a sense of the large-scale impact that the gondola would have on bouldering in LCC.

About Goodbye Gondola Visualization:

Goodbye Gondola Map

The map feature of the visualization includes three varying data point to represent the climbing areas, affected boulders, and gondola towers. To interact with the map, simply hover over the various data points for more information, zoom in and out to narrow the view, or click on an area to zoom to that area. When you zoom in, or click on an area, the table and info chart data will change dynamically to the area selected or the data within the zoomed view.

Areas

Climbing areas are groups of boulders, where each boulder can be described as being in an area. For example, the split boulder is in Five Mile, which is an area. These are represented by blue rectangles on the map. By hovering over an area, you can see the location and name, as well as total views of the page, meaning the popularity of the area. By clicking on the area, the map will zoom to that area and center on it.

Boulders

Boulders are large chunks of rock, in the case of LCC they are more specifically granite chunks. Each boulder has various boulder problems on them, which is a unique path up the boulder. Each problem is graded on a V-Scale. The V-Scale ranges from V0, being the easiest, to V16, being the hardest offered in LCC. The V-Scale is universal to all bouldering across the world. The boulders that will be destroyed due to the gondola construction are represented on the map as grey circles.

Gondola Towers

The gondola towers are large metal poles, around 10 feet in diameter, that will be the support beams for the gondola line. There are a total of 22 towers, proposed to be constructed starting at the base of LCC to the two ski resorts at the top. These towers are represented by a red rectangle nested inside of a cirle, meant to show the area of impact around the tower.

Goodbye Gondola Table/Chart

The chart and table feature of the visualization show the current selection data, displaying the total boulder problems and affected boulder problems in the stacked bar chart, as well as information on each boulder problem in the table. Here, it is easy to see the total impact of the gondola construction, as well as the impact to certain areas by utlizing the drop down menu to select and area, or by clicking an area in the map view.

Bar Chart

The bar chart displays the total boulders of each V grade in the area. The X-Axis is the V grade of the boulder and the Y-Axis is the count of each boulder. In the chart, it can be seen how many of the boulders in the current selection are affected based on the stacked bar chart and tooltip. The red portion of the bar represents the affected boulders and the grey is the unaffected boulder for each V grade in the selected area.

Table

The table displays the same selection data as the bar chart, showing the information on the current selection. The table has multiple columns that can be sorted on, showing the highest grade, views per page, ratings etc. for each selection. To interact with the table, you can simply scroll to see which boulders are affected, indicated by red highlight, or sort each column by clicking on the header of that column. When the selection changes, the table changes dynamically to show the current data.

Map Display Switch

The map display switch is used to switch between selections of the current area selected in the drop down, and the data displayed in the current map view. When an area is selected from the drop down, the map is centered on that area, and the table and chart data changes to match that area. However, if the you want to be able to display all of the data in the current view window, not just the selected area, it is not possible without the switch. The switch allows you to select and area, and then switch from an area selection to the data in the map view, which could be multiple areas, not just the current selected one. When the switch is on, the current map view data is selected, and when it is off, the area in the drop down, or selected on the map is the selection.
